Most marketing leaders talk about scaling. Fewer have done it twice, inside companies that went on to be acquired. Here is what I learned.

## The first time, you are figuring it out

At Secureworks, I was part of a team navigating rapid growth inside a company that was building the managed security services category in real time. The market did not fully understand what we were selling yet. That meant every piece of messaging, every analyst conversation, every sales tool had to do double duty: explain the category and win the deal simultaneously.

That experience taught me something I have carried ever since. When you are early in a category, your most important GTM asset is not your demand generation program. It is your ability to make the market believe the problem you solve is real, urgent, and unsolved by anyone else.

## The second time, you have a blueprint

By the time I joined PhishLabs, I knew what good looked like. I also knew that a blueprint only gets you so far. Every company has a different sales motion, a different buyer, a different set of competitive dynamics. The framework transfers. The execution has to be rebuilt from scratch.

What I did at PhishLabs that I would do again at any early-stage cybersecurity company: I started with positioning before I started with pipeline. We spent the first months getting crystal clear on who we were for, what we were against, and why it mattered now. Only then did we build the demand engine on top of that foundation.

The result was a 164% increase in marketing-sourced ARR in a single year.

## What both exits had in common

In both cases, the acquisition did not happen because the company was well-marketed. It happened because the company had built something genuinely valuable. But in both cases, marketing shaped how that value was perceived — by analysts, by press, by potential acquirers — in ways that made the outcome more likely and the valuation stronger.

That is the real job of a marketing leader at a growth-stage company. Not just pipeline. Not just brand. The job is to make the company's value legible to every audience that matters.

## What I am thinking about next

I am looking for my next opportunity to do this again — at a cybersecurity company in GTM build-out mode, where the strategy needs to be invented, not inherited. If that sounds like where you are, I would love to talk.
